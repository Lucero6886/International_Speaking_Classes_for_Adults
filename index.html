🌍 Navigating the World

A B1/B2 Interactive English Speaking Lesson

🎯 Learning Objectives:
  • Use travel vocabulary to describe past and future trips accurately.
  • Express and logically justify opinions on various cultural experiences.
  • Role-play real-life communication situations confidently for traveling.

1. Warm-up ☕

Let's get talking! Read the questions and click to see an example answer.

✈️ 1. What was the last place you traveled to?

"The last place I visited was Kyoto, Japan. I went there last spring with my family to see the cherry blossoms. It was an unforgettable trip."

🏖️ 2. Do you prefer a relaxing beach holiday or an active city tour?

"I definitely prefer active city tours. I love walking around, visiting historical museums, and trying local street food. Sitting on a beach gets a bit boring for me!"

🎒 3. Do you like traveling alone or with friends/family? Why?

"I prefer traveling with friends because we can share the costs and create memories together. However, traveling alone definitely gives you more freedom."

🍽️ 4. Have you ever tried a strange or unusual food while traveling?

"Yes! When I was in Thailand, I tried fried insects. I was hesitant at first, but it was very crunchy and honestly just tasted like salty chips."

🌟 5. What is your ultimate dream destination?

"My absolute dream destination is Iceland. I really want to witness the Northern Lights in person and soak in the natural geothermal hot springs."

2. Key Vocabulary 📖

Review these useful travel words. Click "See Example" to understand how to use them contextually.

Itinerary (n.)
A detailed plan or route of a journey.
"Our itinerary for Paris is packed; it includes the Eiffel Tower on Monday and the Louvre Museum on Tuesday."
Local Cuisine (n.)
The traditional food of a specific region.
"When you visit Italy, you absolutely must try the local cuisine; authentic pasta is amazing."
Off the beaten path (idm.)
A place not popular with tourists.
"We decided to ignore the guidebooks and found a charming, quiet village entirely off the beaten path."
Breathtaking (adj.)
Astonishingly beautiful or magnificent.
"The panoramic view from the summit of the mountain was absolutely breathtaking."
Culture Shock (n.)
Confusion felt by someone visiting a new society.
"Moving to Tokyo caused a significant degree of culture shock at first, but I gradually adapted."
Souvenir (n.)
A thing kept as a reminder of a place.
"I bought a beautifully carved wooden elephant as a souvenir from my trip to India."
Accommodation (n.)
A room, building, or lodging where someone stays.
"We have already booked our flights, but we still need to secure our accommodation."
Jet lag (n.)
Extreme tiredness after a flight across time zones.
"I suffered from terrible jet lag for three days after flying directly from London to Sydney."

3. Speaking Practice 🗣️

Practice answering these questions out loud. If you get stuck, click the question header to open the Idea Bank and Sample Answers.

1. What are three things you always pack in your suitcase? Easy
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Electronics: Phone charger, power bank, headphones, camera.
  • Comfort: Neck pillow, comfortable walking shoes, sunglasses.
  • Health: Basic medicine, sunscreen, hand sanitizer.
"I always make sure to pack my phone charger, comfortable walking shoes, and a pair of sunglasses."
"There are three absolute essentials I never travel without. First, a power bank because I use my phone constantly for maps and taking photos. Second, comfortable walking shoes because I love exploring new cities on foot. Finally, I always bring basic medicines just in case I feel under the weather in a foreign country."
2. Do you prefer staying in hotels or renting apartments (like Airbnb)? Why? Easy
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Hotels: Room service, daily cleaning, swimming pools, feeling of luxury.
  • Apartments: Kitchen to cook food, more space, feeling like a local resident, often cheaper for groups.
"I personally prefer Airbnb because I enjoy having a kitchen and feeling like a true local."
"It really depends on the nature of the trip. For a short weekend getaway, I love hotels because you don't have to worry about cleaning and you can order room service. But for longer trips, I definitely prefer renting an apartment. Having a kitchen saves a lot of money, and it gives you a much better feel for the local neighborhood culture."
3. How do you prepare before visiting a new country? Easy
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Research: Read travel blogs, watch YouTube vlogs, read reviews.
  • Logistics: Check visa requirements, exchange currency, purchase travel insurance.
  • Language: Learn basic survival phrases like 'Hello', 'Please', and 'Thank you'.
"I usually read various travel blogs and make a comprehensive list of places I want to see."
"Before visiting a new destination, I usually do extensive research. I verify visa requirements first to avoid issues. Then, I watch YouTube vlogs to gather visual ideas for my itinerary. I also make an effort to learn a few basic phrases in the local language, which the locals always seem to appreciate greatly."
4. Have you ever experienced 'culture shock'? Describe it. Medium
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Social norms: Different ways of greeting, eating habits, tipping culture.
  • Environment: Intense traffic, high noise levels, strict public transport rules.
  • Feelings: Confused, overwhelmed, fascinated, occasionally homesick.
"Yes, when I visited India, the chaotic traffic and incredibly spicy food gave me a bit of culture shock initially."
"Absolutely. When I first traveled to Japan, the profound silence on public transport truly shocked me. Where I'm from, trains are loud and people talk constantly on their phones. It felt quite strange at first, but after a few days, I really started to appreciate how peaceful and respectful the culture was."
5. Tell me about a bad travel experience you had. What went wrong? Medium
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Transportation: Missed connecting flights, lost luggage, severely delayed trains.
  • Health/Safety: Food poisoning, getting completely lost, getting scammed by taxis.
  • Weather: Heavy rain all week, unexpected snowstorms ruining plans.
"Once, the airline completely lost my luggage, and I had to wear the exact same clothes for three days straight."
"My absolute worst experience was in Italy during a summer holiday. I got terrible food poisoning from some undercooked seafood. I spent three miserable days stuck in my hotel room while my friends went out sightseeing. On top of that, it rained the entire time! It was incredibly frustrating then, but it's a funny story to tell now."
6. What is the most 'breathtaking' place you have ever seen? Medium
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Nature: Towering mountains, vast oceans, massive waterfalls, protected national parks.
  • Architecture: Ancient historical ruins, massive modern skyscrapers, beautiful detailed temples.
  • Details: Describe the vibrant colors, the sheer scale, the unique atmosphere.
"The Grand Canyon in the USA was the most breathtaking place I've seen simply because of its massive size."
"For me, it was witnessing the Swiss Alps for the very first time. We took a scenic train up the mountains, and when we finally stepped out, the view was simply unbelievable. The air was incredibly crisp, and the snow-capped peaks against the bright blue sky looked exactly like a painting. It was truly a breathtaking moment."
7. Should tourists try to learn the local language before visiting? Opinion
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Yes: Shows respect, helps tremendously in emergencies, fosters a better connection with locals.
  • No: English is widely spoken globally, AI translation apps exist, it's too hard to learn quickly.
  • Middle ground: Just learn basic polite phrases (Hello, Thank you, Excuse me).
"In my opinion, you don't need to be fluent, but you should definitely learn basic words out of respect."
"I strongly believe that tourists should learn at least a little bit of the language. You certainly don't need to have a deep philosophical conversation, but saying 'Hello' and 'Thank you' in the local tongue makes a huge difference. It shows genuine respect for the culture, and I've found that locals are usually much friendlier when they see you making an effort."
8. Do you think traveling changes a person? How? Opinion
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Perspective: Opens your mind, destroys unfair stereotypes, makes you appreciate your own home more.
  • Skills: Improves problem-solving abilities, makes you far more independent and socially confident.
"Yes, traveling makes you much more open-minded because you experience totally different ways of living."
"I definitely think extensive travel changes people. It constantly pushes you out of your safe comfort zone and teaches you how to solve unexpected problems independently. More importantly, seeing firsthand how other people live effectively breaks down stereotypes. You quickly realize that despite superficial cultural differences, people around the world share very similar core values."
9. Is 'eco-tourism' (sustainable travel) important? Why or why not? Opinion
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Pros: Protects fragile environments, supports local community businesses, preserves nature for future generations.
  • Examples: Avoiding single-use plastics, choosing to stay in eco-lodges, not disturbing natural wildlife.
"Yes, it is absolutely crucial because mass commercial tourism is actively destroying beautiful natural sites."
"I think eco-tourism is becoming extremely important. Mass tourism causes severe pollution and directly harms local environments. If we want future generations to enjoy incredible places like coral reefs and rainforests, we must learn to travel responsibly. This means actively supporting small local businesses and consciously minimizing our carbon footprint while exploring."
10. How do you think travel will change in the next 20 years? Opinion
💡 Idea Bank:
  • Technology: Immersive virtual reality (VR) tourism, supersonic faster planes, flawless AI live translators.
  • Space: Commercial space travel becoming normalized for wealthy tourists.
  • Environment: Stricter global rules on flying due to worsening climate change.
"I predict that virtual reality will soon let people 'visit' global landmarks without ever leaving their homes."
"In the next 20 years, I genuinely believe technology will completely revolutionize travel. We might have discreet AI translation earpieces that work perfectly in real-time, effectively eliminating all language barriers. Additionally, because of the climate crisis, physical flights might become much more heavily taxed and expensive, so a lot of people might opt to travel virtually using advanced VR technology instead."

4. Real-life Role-play 🎭

Grab a partner (or your teacher) and practice navigating these real-world scenarios.

🏨 Scenario A: The Lost Reservation

Situation: You have just arrived at your hotel after an exhausting 10-hour flight. The receptionist informs you that they cannot find your booking in their system.

Student A: Exhausted Tourist Student B: Hotel Receptionist
  • Tourist: "Hi, I have a reservation under the name..." / "Here is my confirmation email on my phone." / "Could you please double-check the system?" / "What are my options if you're fully booked tonight?"
  • Receptionist: "Good evening. Can I have your last name and passport, please?" / "I apologize, but I don't see it in the current system." / "Did you book through a third-party agency?" / "Please take a seat; let me speak to my manager."

🍽️ Scenario B: Seeking Local Advice

Situation: You are walking in a completely new city and want to eat something highly traditional. You stop a friendly-looking local resident on the street to ask for recommendations.

Student A: Hungry Tourist Student B: Local Resident
  • Tourist: "Excuse me, I'm so sorry to bother you." / "Do you happen to know a good place to eat around this neighborhood?" / "I'm looking for authentic local cuisine, definitely not a tourist trap." / "How do I get there from here?"
  • Local: "No problem at all! Yes, there's a fantastic spot just two streets away." / "You absolutely have to try the [local dish]." / "It's a bit spicy, do you handle spicy food well?" / "Just walk straight and turn left at the next corner."

5. Fluency Builder 🚀

Use these linking words and sentence starters to sound much more natural. Click to flip the flashcard and see an example.

One reason is that...
"One reason is that international hotels are generally more secure."
On the other hand...
"Air travel is extremely fast. On the other hand, it causes high pollution."
To be completely honest...
"To be completely honest, I suffered terrible jet lag on my last trip."
For instance...
"Many popular places suffer from over-tourism; for instance, Venice."

"I definitely prefer hot, tropical climates over cold, snowy ones."
Looking back...
"Looking back, getting lost in the streets of Rome was actually really fun."
